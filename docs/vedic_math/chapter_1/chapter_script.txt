CHAPTER 1 SCRIPT — “COMPLETING THE WHOLE”
1. EXPLAIN — “Here’s the concept”
Teacher spoken script

“Today we begin with one of the most powerful ideas in Vedic Mathematics: completing the whole.

A ‘whole’ means a complete friendly number like 10, 20, 30, 40. When numbers join to make a whole, addition becomes fast and neat.

For example, if I say 24 + 26, I do not think of long addition first. I notice something special:

4 and 6 make 10.
So 24 and 26 together make 50.

That is the big idea of this chapter:

look for what completes 10,

look for what completes the next multiple of 10,

and use that to add mentally.”

The manual emphasizes that Vedic Mathematics connects to natural mental functions such as “completing a whole,” and that it is fundamentally a mental system.

This is exactly how the text explains the ten-point circle and its use for adding and taking away. 

Manual 1

Teacher spoken explanation: deficiency

“Now another idea: deficiency.
A number may be close to the next ten, but just a little below it.

Example:

38 is close to 40

and it is 2 below 40

So if I want to add 38 + 5, I first use 2 from the 5 to reach 40, and then 3 is left.
So the answer is 43.”

This method is directly shown in the lesson using 38 + 5. 

Manual 1

Board diagram 3 — Number line jump
38 ---- 40 -------- 43
   +2       +3

Then say:

“First complete the whole. Then add the rest.”

Teacher spoken explanation: mental addition with carry

“Now suppose the answer crosses into the next ten.
Example: 56 + 26.

I think:

50 + 20 = 70

6 + 6 = 12

70 + 12 = 82

So 56 + 26 = 82.”

This is the chapter’s exact mental-addition model. 

Manual 1

Board diagram 4 — Mental carry model
56 + 26
= (50 + 20) + (6 + 6)
= 70 + 12
= 82

Then say:

“We are not avoiding carries. We are handling them mentally in a calm way.”

Teacher spoken explanation: adding near a base

“Some numbers are just below a base like 10, 20, 30, 40.
Examples: 9, 19, 29, 38.

These are easy because we can use addition and subtraction together.

Example:
33 + 9
= 33 + 10 - 1
= 43 - 1
= 42

Example:
66 + 19
= 66 + 20 - 1
= 86 - 1
= 85”

This method is explicitly taught in section 1.6.

5. CHECK — “Right! Because…” / “Not quite, here’s why…”

Use these exact teacher feedback lines.

When the student is correct

“Right, because you completed the next ten first.”

“Correct, because you saw the deficiency and used it well.”

“Yes, that is efficient. You grouped the numbers into a whole.”

“Exactly. You added the base and then adjusted.”

“Well done. You handled the carry mentally.”

When the student is partly correct

“You spotted the next ten correctly, but you forgot to add the leftover.”

“Good start. 38 is close to 40, but how much below 40 is it?”

“You added 20 for 19, but did you remember to take 1 away?”

“You subtracted 40 for 38, but what must you add back?”

“You grouped numbers, but try to choose the pair that makes 10 first.”

When the student is incorrect

“Not quite. Let’s go back to the friendly number.”

“Pause. What whole are we trying to complete?”

“Check the deficiency again. Is the number 1 below, 2 below, or 3 below?”

“Try splitting tens and ones separately.”

“Look again: which two numbers can make 10?”

Suggested full classroom flow
Opening

“Today we are training our mind to spot friendly numbers. We are not just adding numbers; we are looking for structure.”

Middle

start with pairs that make 10,

move to completing the next ten,

then mental carry,

then grouping several numbers,

then add/subtract near a base.

This sequence matches the lesson’s progression from ten-point circle and completion, to deficiency, to mental addition, to addition/subtraction near a base.

Closing summary script

“Chapter 1 teaches one big habit: look for the whole.
If numbers can make 10, make 10.
If a number is close to 20, 30, 40, or 50, move to that whole first.
If several numbers are being added, group them into wholes.
If a number is just below a base like 9, 19, or 38, use addition and subtraction together.”
